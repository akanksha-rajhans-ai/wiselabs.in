The Problem

Machines see characters, not meaning

Humans instantly know Car = Automobile, that King ↔ Queen are related, that Doctor → Hospital connects. A computer sees "king" as K-I-N-G. Four characters. Binary in memory. No meaning.

Machines are incredible at math. Terrible at language. The solution: convert language into numbers that preserve meaning.

Slide 3 of 11 — Map of Meaning
Building a Map of Meaning

Distance = similarity

On a real map, Mumbai is close to Pune. Distance is meaningful. Now imagine a map of ideas — where Dog is close to Cat, King is close to Queen, Programming is close to Software Engineering.

Every word gets coordinates in this space. Those coordinates are its embedding.

Meaning becomes geometry. The actual numbers don't matter — the relationships between them do.

ANIMALS CLUSTER

Slide 5 of 11 — Embeddings
Embeddings

Not all vectors are embeddings

I can write [7.3, -4.8, 15.2] right now — that's a vector, but it means nothing.

An embedding is produced by a trained neural network that learned from billions of words. The numbers encode learned meaning, not randomness.

Think of embeddings as compressed meaning — thousands of words and relationships compressed into a list of numbers a computer can reason about mathematically.

Slide 6 of 11 — How Models Learn
How Models Learn

Learning meaning from patterns

Models process enormous text corpora and repeatedly predict missing words — learning which concepts cluster together through pure pattern recognition. Do this billions of times and remarkable relationships emerge.

Discovered without being told

Paris → France  =  Tokyo → Japan
King − Man + Woman ≈ Queen
Doctor → Hospital = Teacher → School

These relationships were never explicitly programmed. They emerge from statistical patterns in training data.

Slide 8 of 11 — Cosine Similarity
Similarity Search

Comparing meaning with cosine similarity

Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors — not their size. Two sentences can differ in length but mean the same thing. They'll point in the same direction.

Formula
cos(θ) = (A·B) / (‖A‖×‖B‖)
1.0
identical
0.8
strongly related
0.5
somewhat
0.0
unrelated
ANGLE = SIMILARITY
θ≈10° — "fast cars" vs "speedy vehicles" → very similar
θ≈72° — "fast cars" vs "banana bread" → unrelated
Small angle = similar direction = similar meaning

Slide 9 of 11 — Semantic Search
Semantic Search

Match meaning, not words

You search: "How do I reset my password?"
The document says: "Account credential recovery process."

Traditional keyword search fails — no words match. Semantic search succeeds because both embed to similar vectors. Same intent = same direction in space.

This is why modern AI search feels so much smarter. It matches meaning, not characters.

Query: "How do I reset my password?"

Slide 11 of 11 — Takeaways
Key Takeaways + Interview Prep

The three things to remember

1️⃣

Machines understand numbers, not words

The entire field of NLP is built on converting language into math.

2️⃣

Embeddings are learned, meaningful vectors

Neural networks compress semantic relationships into dense numerical representations.

3️⃣

Similar meaning → similar vectors → close in space

This powers RAG, AI search, recommendations, and more.

🎯 If asked in an interview
What is an embedding?
A dense vector representation where semantically similar items are close together in vector space.
Why cosine similarity?
Measures directional similarity while being insensitive to vector magnitude — correct for text of varying length.
Where are embeddings used?
Semantic search, RAG pipelines, recommendations, clustering, classification, document Q&A.
