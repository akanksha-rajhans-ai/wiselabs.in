The Problem

Machines see characters, not meaning

Humans instantly understand that Car and Automobile mean the same thing. That King and Queen are related. That Doctor and Hospital connect.

A computer sees the word "king" as just four characters : K, I, N, G, or even more fundamentally, as binary numbers in memory. No royalty. No relationship to queen. No meaning whatsoever.

Machines are incredible at math. They're terrible at understanding language directly. The solution: convert language into numbers that preserve meaning.

03 / 11
Building a Map of Meaning

Distance = similarity

On a real map, Mumbai is close to Pune. Distance is meaningful. Now imagine a map of ideas.

On this map, Dog is close to Cat. King is close to Queen. Programming is close to Software Engineering. The closer two concepts, the more related they are.

Every word gets coordinates : just like GPS. Those coordinates are its embedding.

Meaning becomes geometry. The actual numbers don't matter — the relationships between them do.

04 / 11
Vectors

A vector is just a list of numbers

That's it. Nothing scary. [2, 5, 7] is a vector. So is [-1.2, 3.8, 9.4].

Modern AI models use vectors with hundreds or thousands of numbers. Think of them as coordinates in a giant mathematical space coordinates that tell us where a word lives relative to all other words.

05 / 11
Embeddings

Not all vectors are embeddings

I can generate [7.3, -4.8, 15.2] right now. That's a vector but it means nothing.

An embedding is a vector produced by a trained neural network. The network learned from billions of words which appear together, which sentences mean similar things, which concepts connect.

The result: a vector whose numbers encode learned meaning, not randomness.

Think of embeddings as compressed meaning. Thousands of words and relationships compressed into a list of numbers that a computer can reason about mathematically.

06 / 11
How Models Learn

Learning meaning from patterns

Models don't get programmed with facts. They process enormous text corpora and repeatedly predict missing words, learning which concepts cluster together through pure pattern recognition.

Given: "The cat sat on the ____" — the model learns "mat" is far more likely than "airplane". Do this billions of times and the model discovers surprisingly rich relationships.

What it discovers without being told

Paris → France  =  Tokyo → Japan
King → Man  ≈  Queen → Woman
Doctor → Hospital  =  Teacher → School

These relationships were never explicitly programmed. They emerge from statistical patterns in training data. That's what makes embeddings powerful.

Similarity Search

Comparing meaning with cosine similarity

Now every word, sentence, and document is a vector. How do we find which ones are similar?

Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors not their size. Two sentences can be different lengths but mean the same thing. They'll point in the same direction.

Formula
cos(θ) = (A · B) / (‖A‖ × ‖B‖)
1.0
identical
0.8
strongly related
0.5
somewhat related
0.0

Semantic Search

Match meaning, not words

You search: "How do I reset my password?"
The document says: "Account credential recovery process."

Traditional keyword search fails, none of the words match. Semantic search succeeds because both query and document embed to similar vectors. They express the same intent.

This is why modern AI powered search feels so much smarter than old school search. It's matching meaning, not characters.

The embedding model doesn't care about exact wording. It cares about the direction the text points in vector space, and similar intents point in similar directions.

Key Takeaways + Interview Prep

The three things to remember

1️⃣

Machines understand numbers, not words

The whole field of NLP is built on finding ways to convert language into math.

2️⃣

Embeddings are learned, meaningful vectors

Neural networks compress semantic relationships into dense numerical representations.

3️⃣

Similar meaning → similar vectors → close in space

Semantic search finds meaning. This powers RAG, AI search, recommendations, and more.

🎯 Engineer Notes — If asked in an interview
What is an embedding?
A dense vector representation where semantically similar items are located close together in vector space.
Why cosine similarity?
Measures directional similarity while being insensitive to vector magnitude, correct signal for text of varying length.
Where are embeddings used?
Semantic search, RAG pipelines, recommendation systems, clustering, classification, document Q&A.
